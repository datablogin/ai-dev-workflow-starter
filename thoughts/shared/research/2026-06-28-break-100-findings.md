# Findings: "Break 100" Stroke-Gap Diagnosis

## Headline

For a golfer who shoots ~100 (≈25 handicap), **approach play is the
highest-leverage place to practice** — by a wide margin, for both the
"just break 100" target and the longer-term stretch target. It accounts for
roughly **half** of all the strokes available in each tier.

## How this was produced

Numbers below are generated by the tool, not hand-typed, so the report is
regenerable:

```
python3 break100.py data/benchmarks.csv
```

Method: for each game category, gap = `target_strokes_gained − your_strokes_gained`,
where both are strokes gained per round vs the PGA Tour baseline. A positive gap
is strokes you could save by reaching that target tier. Source data and full
provenance: `data/benchmarks.csv` and `data/SOURCES.md`.

## Results

### Target: 20-handicap (just break 100)

| Category | Strokes saveable |
|---|---|
| **Approach** | **+1.5**  ← highest leverage |
| Off the tee | +0.8 |
| Short game | +0.5 |
| Putting | +0.4 |
| **TOTAL** | **+3.2** |

### Target: 15-handicap (stretch)

| Category | Strokes saveable |
|---|---|
| **Approach** | **+3.0**  ← highest leverage |
| Off the tee | +1.5 |
| Short game | +1.0 |
| Putting | +0.7 |
| **TOTAL** | **+6.2** |

## What this means for practice

1. **Spend the most practice time on approach shots** (full-swing iron/hybrid
   shots into the green, including par-3 tee shots). It is the single biggest
   gap to both targets — ~1.5 strokes toward breaking 100 and ~3.0 toward a
   15-handicap, about half the total available improvement either way.
2. **Off-the-tee is a clear second** (+0.8 / +1.5). Worth attention after
   approach, but not the place to start.
3. **Short game and putting are the smallest gaps** (+0.5/+0.4 and +1.0/+0.7).
   This is *not* "putting doesn't matter" — it's that, relative to a golfer one
   or two tiers better, you lose the fewest strokes there. Diminishing returns
   for a 25-handicap compared with approach work.
4. The ranking is **identical for both targets**, so the practice priority is
   stable whether the goal is to just break 100 or to push on toward a
   15-handicap. Approach first, then driving.

## Caveats — read before acting (descriptive, not causal)

- **This is a population average, not your game.** The benchmarks describe where
  a *typical* 25-handicap loses strokes versus a typical 20-/15-handicap. They
  are a strong prior about where the leverage usually is — not a measurement of
  your own swing. If your driving is genuinely wild or your putting genuinely
  poor, your personal gaps will differ. Tracking your own rounds would refine
  this.
- **No causal claim.** "Approach is the largest available stroke saving" is not
  "fixing your approach *will* make you break 100." Improvement also depends on
  practice quality, consistency, and course management.
- **Single source for the headline table.** Values come from one published
  table (Golfity, attributed to Mark Broadie's research, vs Tour baseline) to
  keep category definitions and the baseline consistent. The pivot research had
  intended Shot Scope as primary; its exact numbers were not machine-extractable
  this session. Swapping in better data later is a per-row edit to
  `data/benchmarks.csv` — see `data/SOURCES.md` ("How to update").
- **"Short game" == "around the green"** in the source table.
